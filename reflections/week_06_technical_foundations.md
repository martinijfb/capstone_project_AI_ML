## Submission (plain text, ~675 words)

**Technical justification for the current BBO approach**

The core of my approach is a "validate-then-trust" pipeline: every model fit goes through leave-one-out cross-validation before its predictions are allowed to influence a query. Models that fail to beat the baseline RMSE (Y.std()) are excluded entirely. The framework is per-function adaptive: F1, F4, and F8 use different decision branches because they have different response landscapes. The boundary-consensus rule and outlier-suggestion filter are my own additions, designed for failure modes I observed in early weeks (models extrapolating to boundary corners or producing wildly off-cluster suggestions). The point is that no single model gets to make the decision unilaterally; suggestions only count if they survive cross-validation against the data and agree with at least one other model family.

**Academic papers and literature**

I've drawn most directly from the course's treatment of Bayesian optimisation (Frazier's tutorial framing) and Gaussian processes (Rasmussen & Williams). The Matern kernel family with adjustable smoothness is the most directly applied idea: this week I expanded my GP family from ν=2.5 to include ν=0.5 (very rough) and ν=1.5 (intermediate), treating kernel smoothness as a hyperparameter that cross-validation chooses rather than something I hard-code. The no-free-lunch theorem (Wolpert & Macready, 1997) is the conceptual backbone of my per-function strategy: since no single optimiser dominates across all eight functions, letting cross-validation pick the surrogate per function is more defensible than committing to one method up front. Eriksson et al's TuRBO paper (NeurIPS 2019) is on my radar as a potential upgrade for plateauing functions like F8, where my deterministic ensemble argmax under-explores.

**Third-party libraries**

scikit-learn is the central library: every sklearn model family (Ridge, KNN, Random Forest, SVR, GradientBoosting, GaussianProcessRegressor) is wrapped in GridSearchCV with LeaveOneOut, so the validate-first principle is enforced by the API rather than by convention. PyTorch handles the neural-network surrogates. I chose it over TensorFlow because torch.autograd makes extracting dY/dx at the current best point a one-line operation, and that gradient signal is how I get a directional hint from the NN even on weeks when its overall accuracy doesn't beat the ensemble. NumPy and scipy.spatial.distance handle ensemble averaging and outlier filtering. Matplotlib with the Wong colourblind palette for figures. Jupyter nbformat and nbclient orchestrate the weekly notebook executions programmatically. The most recent infrastructure improvement was switching to uv-managed native arm64 Python on M-series hardware, which removed a multiprocessing instability that had been killing kernels and roughly tripled BLAS throughput.

**GitHub repo documentation**

The repo is organised around the weekly cadence. Each `notebooks/week_XX.ipynb` has a decision-markdown cell per function explaining the chosen branch and the per-dimension justification. `weekly_queries/week_XX.md` archives the formatted submission and the method used per function. `suggestions/suggestions_for_week_(XX+1).md` contains conditional strategies for the next week, branched on whether the result improves, plateaus, or drops. `reflections/` holds the discussion-board posts. The `README` has a section per function summarising current state and the strategy I would defend if asked. A peer reading the repo cold should be able to follow not just what I did but why I chose a given branch over its alternatives.

**Future sources**

The biggest gap in my toolkit is principled acquisition functions. My current approach is a deterministic ensemble argmax, essentially greedy exploitation. Published alternatives I plan to test: GP-UCB with kappa scheduling (Srinivas et al, ICML 2010), Expected Improvement (Jones et al, 1998), and TuRBO's trust-region approach (Eriksson et al, NeurIPS 2019). BoTorch is the PyTorch-native library that implements these cleanly and would slot in without rewriting the surrogate side. ENTMOOT from the Computational Optimisation Group at Imperial uses gradient-boosted trees as surrogates instead of GPs, which would let me test whether my GP-heavy choice is actually well-suited to these landscapes. The NeurIPS 2020 BBO Challenge winning solutions (notably the JetBrains TuRBO-based entry) are worth studying for how trust-region exploration is implemented in practice, even though their problem regime (hyperparameter tuning) is different from the unknown-function setup here.
