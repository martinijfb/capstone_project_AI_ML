# Week 05 Reflection — Deep Learning Building Blocks

## Portal prompts (6)

1. How did the ideas of hierarchical feature learning influence the way you thought about structuring or refining your optimisation strategy this round?
2. You saw how breakthroughs such as AlexNet and ImageNet classification reshaped expectations in AI. What parallels do you see between those leaps in performance and the incremental improvements you make in your capstone submissions?
3. When training neural networks, people often weigh trade-offs between depth, complexity and training efficiency. Did you encounter similar trade-offs in deciding whether to explore widely or exploit known promising regions in your queries?
4. Reflecting on the building blocks of neural networks (inputs, activations, loss, gradients, weight updates), which of these concepts helped you think differently about how your model learns from the data you've accumulated so far?
5. Module 16 also introduced PyTorch and TensorFlow as different frameworks for building and scaling models. If you were to frame your current optimisation approach in terms of a 'framework', would it be closer to rapid prototyping and flexibility or to structured, production-ready design? Why?
6. In the guest interview, Giovanni Liotta discussed industry applications of deep learning in sport. How might reflecting on real-world deep learning use cases inform the way you benchmark success in your own capstone challenge?

Word limit: **700**. Date: 2026-04-22.

---

## Submission answer (plain text)

**Q1.** My pipeline does something analogous, just at a much shallower scale. The low-level features are per-dimension correlations and RF importance rankings. The mid-level ones are per-model argmax suggestions across seven or eight model families. The high-level decision is the per-dim hybrid that combines ensemble agreement, Y-weighted top-4 centroids, and boundary-consensus checks. Each layer summarises the one below. This week it helped on F8: half the models hit boundaries and got dropped, the rest produced per-dim spreads that classified each dimension as STRONG, moderate or weak, and that classification told the hybrid where to use the ensemble vs fall back to the centroid.

**Q2.** AlexNet was a step change because data, hardware and architecture all crossed thresholds at once. My capstone progresses one point per function per week with the same families, so leaps are rare. The closest thing to a real leap was F4 in the previous round, which had been stuck for three weeks and then jumped substantially once the ensemble's step direction was right. Big gains in BBO seem to need either a regime change (a new model becoming dominant, like GB on F3 once I removed the outlier) or stumbling into a region the previous models had missed. Most of the time it's incremental, with small steps adding up.

**Q3.** Same shape as explore vs exploit, exactly. Each function sits somewhere on that axis. F5 has been pure exploitation, climbing the same direction four rounds in a row. F1 sits at the other end but it isn't pure exploration either: no regression model beats baseline, so I built informed exploration around it (a sign classifier on Y, a log-magnitude regressor on log|Y|, a combined score, and balanced Voronoi targeting whichever quadrant has the fewest samples). F2 tried exploration last week (the low-x2 ridge hypothesis) and paid the cost when it failed. The neural-net trade-off is similar: a deeper network can model more complex patterns but needs more data and risks overfitting; a wider exploration query can find new optima but wastes a slot if the current direction is still climbing. I default to the safer move, an RMSE-weighted ensemble of cross-validated models, and only deviate when there's specific evidence to justify a risk.

**Q4.** Gradients, by far. Once I had `torch.autograd.backward` extracting dY/dx at each current-best point, I started reading per-dim gradients alongside correlations. On F4 the gradient said push x1 down and x2 up, and the ensemble agreed, so I treated that as confirmation. On F8 the gradient said push x5 up but the ensemble didn't agree, which was useful information by itself: the NN's accuracy on that function is below GP-Matern's, so the disagreement is a flag, not a vote. The gradient view also reframed sensitive vs flat dimensions for me. Large gradients mean a small step has big consequences. Near-zero gradients mean the centroid is fine.

**Q5.** Closer to rapid prototyping. The pipeline tries seven model families per function, runs CV grid search on each, applies per-dim consensus rules, builds the hybrid. That's a fast iterate-and-decide loop, not production logic. I'd call it research-grade: modular and reproducible, but missing what you'd add for deployment (input validation, structured config, tests on the boundary-consensus rule). It works because I'm the only consumer and know the failure modes. If I had to hand it off, the next step would be schemas and a small test suite around the per-dim decision logic.

**Q6.** Liotta's Sport Dojo uses deep learning to bring elite-level analysis (player tracking, pose estimation) to amateur coaches via consumer-grade video. What stuck with me wasn't the models but the benchmark. He's not measuring against perfect performance. He's measuring against what amateurs could otherwise access, which is basically nothing structured. My capstone has a similar framing. Perfect optima aren't expected. Success is whether my reasoning process produced queries better than random search would have, and whether each weekly query is a defensible decision under uncertainty. For F1, where the function is essentially zero everywhere we've sampled, success isn't finding a peak this week. It's having a justifiable rationale (balanced Voronoi in the most undersampled quadrant) and a classifier that gets better data to learn from. Useful given the constraints, like Liotta's product.
